The 4 Requirement Types — Know These Cold
Business
High-level statements of goals, objectives, and outcomes the enterprise needs to achieve. The "why." Ex: Reduce customer churn by 15%.
Stakeholder
Needs of a specific stakeholder group relative to the solution. The "what stakeholders need." Ex: Sales reps need to view customer history in one screen.
Solution
Describes features and capabilities the solution must have. Split into Functional (what it does) and Non-Functional (how well it does it).
Transition
Temporary capabilities needed only during transition from current to future state. Ex: Data migration script to move legacy records.
Knowledge Area 01
Business Analysis
Planning & Monitoring
Defines how BA work will be planned, organized, monitored, and governed throughout an initiative. Sets the stage for everything else.
Core Tasks
Plan BA Approach
Defines the overall method—predictive, adaptive, or hybrid—and how BA work will be structured, sequenced, and performed.
Plan Stakeholder Engagement
Identifies who the stakeholders are, analyzes their needs and influence, and determines how to effectively engage each group.
Plan BA Governance
Establishes decision-making processes: who approves requirements, how changes are managed, and how conflicts are escalated.
Plan Information Management
Determines how requirements and BA artifacts will be stored, organized, accessed, and maintained throughout the project.
Identify BA Performance Improvements
Continuously assesses BA activities and outputs for effectiveness and makes adjustments to improve quality and efficiency.
Key Techniques — tap any term to see its definition
Brainstorming Interviews Lessons Learned Mind Mapping Organizational Modelling Process Modelling Risk Analysis Stakeholder List / Map RACI Matrix
Exam Tips
Know the difference between predictive (plan-driven) and adaptive (iterative) approaches and how BA planning differs between them.
Stakeholders with high influence require proactive management regardless of their level of interest.
Governance = decision-making authority + change control process. The BA doesn't own governance but facilitates it.
Performance improvement is ongoing and iterative — it's not a one-time review at project end.
Key Terms
BA Approach
The overall methodology defining when, how, and what BA activities will be performed throughout the initiative.
Governance
Processes defining how decisions are made, who has authority, how changes are requested and approved, and how conflicts are resolved.
Stakeholder Engagement
The ongoing process of identifying, analyzing, and planning appropriate interaction with stakeholders based on their needs and influence.
CDU
Continuing Development Unit. Required for ECBA renewal (21 CDUs every 3 years) to demonstrate ongoing professional development.
Knowledge Area 02
Elicitation &
Collaboration
The active process of drawing out, confirming, and communicating business analysis information. Elicitation is investigative — not just passive collection.
Core Tasks
Prepare for Elicitation
Plans each elicitation activity: defines objectives, selects techniques, prepares questions/materials, and schedules participants.
Conduct Elicitation
Executes the planned activity to draw out information from stakeholders. May reveal stated, unstated, and tacit needs.
Confirm Elicitation Results
Reviews and validates that captured information is accurate and complete. Typically done by sharing notes back with participants.
Communicate BA Information
Ensures the right information reaches the right stakeholders at the right time in a format appropriate to the audience.
Manage Stakeholder Collaboration
Builds and maintains productive working relationships; manages conflict, participation, and buy-in throughout the initiative.
Elicitation Techniques — Know All of These — tap any term
Interviews Workshops / JAD Focus Groups Observation (Active & Passive) Surveys / Questionnaires Prototyping Document Analysis Interface Analysis Brainstorming Collaborative Games Mind Mapping Benchmarking
Exam Tips
Elicitation ≠ requirements gathering. Elicitation actively surfaces tacit and unstated knowledge; gathering is passive.
Passive observation = watching without interacting. Active observation = participating alongside the user.
Vague statements ("user-friendly," "fast") must always be probed for measurable, specific meaning — never accepted as written.
Nominal Group Technique and brainwriting neutralize dominant personalities in group settings.
Confirm Elicitation Results comes BEFORE drafting formal requirements — accuracy first.
Technique Selector
Need tacit knowledge
Open-ended interviews or observation — surface what stakeholders know but can't easily articulate.
Large group input
Surveys for breadth; workshops for depth and consensus.
Stakeholder can't articulate needs
Prototyping — give them something concrete to react to.
Understand current process
Observation or document analysis — see what actually happens, not just what's described.
Dominant personality problem
Brainwriting or Nominal Group Technique to capture independent perspectives.
Knowledge Area 03
Requirements Life
Cycle Management
Managing requirements from creation through retirement — ensuring they stay accurate, traceable, prioritized, and approved as the project evolves.
Core Tasks
Trace Requirements
Documents relationships between requirements and related artifacts (goals, tests, design elements). Enables impact analysis.
Maintain Requirements
Keeps requirements accurate, complete, consistent, and accessible as the initiative progresses and changes occur.
Prioritize Requirements
Determines relative importance and implementation order based on value, cost, risk, and dependencies.
Assess Requirements Changes
Evaluates the impact of proposed changes on related requirements, design components, and project plans before accepting.
Approve Requirements
Obtains formal stakeholder sign-off that requirements are complete and accurate, authorizing the solution team to proceed.
Prioritization Techniques — tap any term
MoSCoW (Must/Should/Could/Won't) Weighted Ranking / Scoring Timeboxing Voting / Dot Voting Buy a Feature Delphi Technique Kano Model
Exam Tips
Always assess impact BEFORE accepting or rejecting a change request — never update requirements first.
Traceability runs both directions: forward (to tests/design) and backward (to business goals).
Approval ≠ sign-off for the sake of it. It formally authorizes action and signals stakeholder confidence in the requirements.
When stakeholders conflict over a requirement, the BA facilitates — not arbitrates. Return to the business need.
MoSCoW is the most common prioritization technique on the exam. Know what each category means.
Key Terms
Traceability
Documented relationships between requirements and other artifacts, enabling completeness checks and change impact analysis.
Dependency
A relationship where one requirement relies on another being implemented first for it to be meaningful.
Baseline
An approved, stable version of requirements at a point in time — the reference point for managing changes.
MoSCoW
Must Have / Should Have / Could Have / Won't Have. A simple prioritization framework for categorizing requirements by necessity.
Knowledge Area 04
Strategy
Analysis
Understands why change is needed, what the future should look like, and how to get there. Bridges business context with solution direction.
Core Tasks
Analyze Current State
Establishes the baseline: existing capabilities, processes, organizational context, and the business need or opportunity driving change.
Define Future State
Describes the desired outcomes: required capabilities, organizational changes, KPIs, and what success looks like.
Assess Risks
Identifies uncertainties that could affect the ability to achieve the future state and determines how to respond to them.
Define Change Strategy
Determines the best approach to move from current to future state: build vs. buy, phasing, resource model, and transition approach.
Key Analytical Techniques — tap any term
SWOT Analysis PESTLE Analysis Root Cause Analysis Gap Analysis Force Field Analysis Benchmarking Business Capability Analysis Decision Analysis Risk Analysis Fishbone / Ishikawa Diagram Five Whys
Exam Tips
Business Need = problem or opportunity justifying investment. Never jump to a solution before understanding the need.
Always do root cause analysis before recommending solutions — symptoms ≠ causes.
SWOT = internal (strengths/weaknesses) + external (opportunities/threats). PESTLE = all external macro factors.
Gap analysis = current state capabilities vs. future state capabilities. The gap defines the scope of change.
KPIs in the future state definition must be specific and measurable — not vague objectives.
Key Terms
Business Need
A problem or opportunity significant enough to justify investment. The core "why" behind any initiative.
Solution Scope
The boundary of what the solution will and will not address — distinct from project scope (the work to be done).
Gap Analysis
Comparison of current state capabilities against desired future state to identify what must change.
Change Strategy
The selected approach for achieving the future state, including build/buy decision, phasing, and transition plan.
KPI
Key Performance Indicator — a specific, measurable metric defining what success looks like in the future state.
Knowledge Area 05
Requirements Analysis
& Design Definition
Structures, models, verifies, and validates requirements — then defines design options for how the solution will meet those needs.
Core Tasks
Specify & Model Requirements
Represents requirements in structured formats (text, diagrams, models) to improve clarity, precision, and stakeholder understanding.
Verify Requirements
Ensures requirements have the right quality characteristics: complete, consistent, correct, feasible, testable, atomic, etc.
Validate Requirements
Confirms requirements will actually deliver the intended business value — checking alignment with the business need.
Define Requirements Architecture
Organizes requirements into a coherent structure that reflects their relationships and supports understanding at different levels.
Define Design Options
Identifies and analyzes different ways the solution could be designed to meet requirements, including trade-offs.
Analyze Potential Value & Recommend Solution
Evaluates design options against value, cost, risk, and feasibility to recommend the best path forward.
Modeling Techniques — tap any term
Use Case Diagrams User Stories Process Models (BPMN) Data Flow Diagrams Entity-Relationship Diagrams State Diagrams Sequence Diagrams Context Diagrams Decision Tables Prototyping Interface Analysis
Exam Tips
Verify = quality check (are requirements well-written?). Validate = value check (will they deliver business value?). These are different.
Atomicity: one requirement = one testable statement. If it can be split, it should be.
Consistency: no two requirements should contradict each other. Conflicts must be resolved before proceeding.
Non-functional requirements describe qualities (performance, security, usability) — not behaviors or functions.
An actor in a use case = any role that interacts with the system, not necessarily a person (can be an external system).
Requirements Quality Characteristics
Atomic
Each requirement contains exactly one discrete, independently testable statement.
Complete
No missing information; the requirement fully describes what is needed with no gaps.
Consistent
Does not contradict any other requirement in the set.
Feasible
Can be implemented within the known constraints of the project (budget, technology, time).
Testable
Can be verified through inspection, testing, or demonstration. Vague language makes requirements untestable.
Unambiguous
Has only one valid interpretation. No pronouns without clear referents, no subjective language.
Knowledge Area 06
Solution
Evaluation
Assesses whether a deployed solution is delivering the intended business value — and identifies what must change when it isn't.
Core Tasks
Measure Solution Performance
Collects and analyzes post-deployment data to determine whether the solution is achieving its intended outcomes and KPIs.
Analyze Performance Measures
Interprets performance data to understand whether gaps exist between expected and actual value, and why.
Assess Solution Limitations
Identifies factors within the solution itself that are preventing it from delivering its full potential value.
Assess Enterprise Limitations
Identifies organizational factors (process, culture, skills, governance) outside the solution that are limiting value realization.
Recommend Actions to Increase Solution Value
Proposes corrective actions—enhancements, process changes, training, retirement—to close the value gap.
Key Techniques — tap any term
Metrics & KPIs Surveys / Questionnaires Observation Focus Groups Root Cause Analysis Interviews Risk Analysis Benchmarking
Exam Tips
A technically correct solution can still fail to deliver value — always investigate organizational/behavioral factors first.
Enterprise limitations = org factors outside the solution (training gaps, unchanged processes, low adoption, culture).
Solution limitations = deficiencies within the solution itself (bugs, missing features, design flaws).
Solution evaluation closes the loop with Strategy Analysis — did we actually achieve the future state we defined?
When value is lower than expected, investigate before recommending. Don't jump to "replace the system."
Key Terms
Solution Performance
The degree to which a solution is achieving the outcomes and KPIs defined in the future state, measured post-deployment.
Enterprise Limitations
Internal organizational factors—culture, process, skills, governance—that cap the value a solution can deliver.
Solution Limitations
Deficiencies within the solution itself that prevent it from fully meeting requirements or delivering intended value.
Value Gap
The difference between expected business value and actual realized value after deployment. The gap drives improvement actions.
Org. Readiness
The degree to which impacted stakeholders have the knowledge, skills, and motivation to successfully adopt the solution.
